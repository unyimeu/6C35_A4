Proposition: A small group of officers accumulate the majority of complaints, yet their allegations are the least likely to be substantiated.

FOR the Proposition

Notes: IsSUB is a calculated field defined as such:
IF STARTSWITH([Board Disposition], "Substantiated") THEN 1 ELSE 0 END

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • D1: Downward trend line
    • Score: 1.5
    • How does this decision help make your visualization persuasive?
      The trend line is a very persuasive element of the visualization. My goal when adding this was to show to the viewer that there is a clear negative relationship between number of allegations and substantiation rate. This makes it visually obvious that officers who accumulate more complaints are less likely to have them proven.
    • What worked well, and what didn't?
      This worked well because the data naturally supports a downward trend. I didn't need to do any manipulation as I relied on the inherent data trends. However, this trend line is somewhat hard to see due to the larger cluster of data points. Maybe it could benefit from being placed in a more obvious way.
    • What other alternatives did you consider, and why did you settle on this one?
      Tableau had many options for different kinds of trend lines such as logarithmic, exponential and power. Despite the data having somewhat of a curve, I still went with just a linear because the overall goal of this line was just to show the general direction the data was going in. A linear line clearly communicates this without over complicating.
  • D2: Including all officers with no minimum allegation filter
    • Score: -0.5
    • How does this decision help make your visualization persuasive?
      By including officers with very few allegations (1-5), the upper-left corner gets packed with high-substantiation-rate dots. Officers with just 1 or 2 allegations can easily land at 1.0 or 0.5, which inflates the visual impression that low-allegation officers have high rates and exaggerates the steepness of the downward trend.
    • What worked well, and what didn't?
      It worked well for persuasion as many of the officers with just a few allegations were substantiated, but not filtering such officers out might have contributed to some unreliable rates.
    • What other alternatives did you consider, and why did you settle on this one?
      I considered filtering to a minimum of 5 or 10 allegations for more honest representation, but keeping all officers in made the downward slope more dramatic and the upper-left cluster more dense. Additionally, I don't have the knowledge about this dataset to determine whether these cases are worth filtering in the first place.
  • D3: Annotating Officer 18530 (73 allegations, 0.3151 rate) and Officer 25470 (9 allegations, 0.8889 rate)
    • Score: 1.5
    • How does this decision help make your visualization persuasive?
      I chose to highlight specific officers to make the current patterns in the data appear more concrete and personal. The viewer sees "73 complaints and only 31% proven" next to "9 complaints and 89% proven" and is able to more-so grasp the disparity. It shows that these are actual individual cases that represent this data.
    • What worked well, and what didn't?
      It worked well because the two officers sit at opposite ends of the chart and thus it tells a contrasting narrative. My proposition now has support from real examples rather than just purely on the trend. What didn't work well is that these annotations only depict two individuals at both ends of the spectrum. It's clearly cherry picking these extreme examples.
    • What other alternatives did you consider, and why did you settle on this one?
      I considered annotating different officers but chose these two because they represent the most dramatic contrast. With one in the upper left with few allegations and a high rate, one in the lower-right with many allegations and a low rate.
  • D4: Title framing "Which Officers are Receiving the Highest Rate of Substantiated (Considered Proven) Allegations"
    • Score: 2
    • How does this decision help make your visualization persuasive?
      The title of every visualization is what first guides the viewers' understanding. For this visualization, I chose to frame the title as a question, thus prompting the reader to search for an answer. In the chart they start immediately looking for the answer and it reinforces the persuasive message. I added "Considered Proven" in parentheses to remind the reader that substantiation means verified misconduct.
    • What worked well, and what didn't?
      It works well because it guides interpretation without being overtly biased. The parenthetical adds gravity to the word "substantiated" for readers unfamiliar with the term. Although the title does get the user searching for an answer, maybe the part about 'A small group of officers" as in the proposition could be communicated clearly in the title to emphasize this aspect of the proposition.
    • What other alternatives did you consider, and why did you settle on this one?
      I considered a neutral title like "Substantiation Rate by Allegation Count" but that doesn't lead the reader toward a conclusion the way a question does.
  • D5: Using person-shaped marks instead of circles
    • Score: 0.5
    • How does this decision help make your visualization persuasive?
      I chose the human figure icons instead of just circles to remind the reader that each dot represents a real officer with real allegations against them. This adds emotional weight and overall raises the stakes of the pattern being shown.
    • What worked well, and what didn't?
      It worked well because the icons are distinct and recognizable even at small sizes. However, they do create more visual clutter than circles would, especially in dense areas of the chart. Thus it gets somewhat difficult to discern just how many people are located at a particular datapoint.
    • What other alternatives did you consider, and why did you settle on this one?
      I considered using standard circles as stated before, which would be cleaner and reduce overlap, but the person shapes add a humanizing layer of meaning that supports the persuasive goal of making the reader care about the pattern. In addition, I considered not measuring individual people at all and rather just showing the trends potentially with a line. But I felt like this representation was able to better communicate the proposition.

AGAINST the Proposition

Note:
The follow calculated fields were used:

  • Overall Avg Time to Sub
    {FIXED : AVG({FIXED [Unique Mos Id] : DATEDIFF('month', MIN([Date Received]), MIN([Substantiated Date]))})}
  • Time Bin:
    INT([Time to First Substantiation] / 12) * 12

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • D1: Filtering to only high-allegation officers (30-75 allegations)
    • Score: -2
    • How does this decision help make your visualization persuasive?
      In this visualization I am considering those with high allegations as 30-75. In reality I just filtered out those with a very high number of allegations as they actually pulled the average further to the right. By filtering only officers with 30-75 allegations, I removed the majority of officers from the dataset who would have dragged the average. This lets me compare a cherry picked subset against the full population average to make it look like high-allegation officers are substantiated faster.
    • What worked well, and what didn't?
      This worked well because readers see the green line sitting to the left of the red line and immediately conclude that the system is actually working well and counters the proposition by showing that people with many allegations are actually being substantiated faster. What didn't work as well is the footnote at the bottom which reveals the filter.
    • What other alternatives did you consider, and why did you settle on this one?
      I considered showing all officers on the same chart, but then there would be no meaningful difference between the two averages. The filter is what creates the persuasive gap between the two reference lines.
  • D2: Two reference lines comparing high-allegation officers vs. all officers
    • Score: -0.5
    • How does this decision help make your visualization persuasive?
      I placed two vertical reference lines. One line is for the filtered group and one line is for all officers. This design choice allows the viewer to be able to directly compare these values just by looking at the visual. The ~6 month gap between the lines visually argues that high-allegation officers get substantiated sooner than average.
    • What worked well, and what didn't?
      The visual comparison is effective as viewers don't need to think hard to get the message. However, the gap is only about 6 months, which is not that dramatic. I had to rely on the annotation calling out "~6 months apart" to make sure readers noticed.
    • What other alternatives did you consider, and why did you settle on this one?
      I considered using only one reference line, but having both creates the comparison that drives the argument. Without the "all officers" line, there would be no benchmark to make the high-allegation group look faster.
  • D3: Title framing "High-Allegation Officers Are Substantiated Earlier Than Average"
    • Score: -1
    • How does this decision help make your visualization persuasive?
      I chose a title that states my desired conclusion as a fact rather than letting the reader interpret the data. This forces the reader to see the chart as confirmation of the claim rather than exploring it with an open mind.
    • What worked well, and what didn't?
      It worked well because readers tend to trust titles and use them to guide their interpretation. The word "earlier" has a positive connotation that makes it sound like the system is working. What doesn't work well is that the title along with the description might be too important and it may be harder to get a message from the visualization without it.
    • What other alternatives did you consider, and why did you settle on this one?
      I considered a neutral title like "Time to First Substantiation for High-Allegation Officers" but that wouldn't push the reader toward the conclusion I wanted. However, this title does the persuasive work better.
  • D4: Histogram with person-shaped marks showing distribution skewed left
    • Score: 0.5
    • How does this decision help make your visualization persuasive?
      I used a histogram so the reader can see that the bulk of high-allegation officers cluster on the left side of the chart. This supports the counter proposition because it means that most of them were substantiated relatively quickly. The tall bars near 0 months make it look like the system acts fast on these officers with repeat allegations.
    • What worked well, and what didn't?
      The left-skewed shape supports the narrative well. The person-shaped marks again, as used in the first visualization, humanize the data. What didn't work as well is that some officers appear at 60-75 months, which undermines the "fast accountability" message, but the density on the left draws attention away from those outliers.
    • What other alternatives did you consider, and why did you settle on this one?
      I considered a scatter plot like the first visualization, but a histogram better emphasizes the concentration of officers on the left side. A scatter plot would have spread the data out and made the skew less obvious. I tried it but it wasn't as effective or organized.
  • D5: X-axis label — "Time (Months) Until Officer was First Held Accountable"
    • Score: -1.5
    • How does this decision help make your visualization persuasive?
      I chose the phrase "Held Accountable" instead of "Substantiated" because it carries a stronger moral connotation. It frames the substantiation as the system actively holding officers responsible rather than a neutral bureaucratic outcome.
    • What worked well, and what didn't?
      It worked well because "held accountable" is emotionally loaded language that reinforces the narrative that the system is functioning. It sounds like justice is being served. Most readers accept axis labels without scrutiny as it is typically just supposed to say what the data is. However, what doesn't work is that it may not be the correct technical language to use for this situation as substantiated may not necessarily mean held accountable exactly.
    • What other alternatives did you consider, and why did you settle on this one?
      I considered using "Time to First Substantiation" which is more technically accurate, but it's dry and doesn't carry the same persuasive weight. "Held Accountable" does rhetorical work that a neutral label wouldn't.

Final Reflection

When designing these two visualizations, it showed me how thin the line between persuasion and deception really is. The first visualization was straightforward because after exploring the data, I saw that the data naturally supported the downward trend. I just needed a trend line and some annotations to make the pattern clear. The second was much harder because I was arguing against what the data naturally shows, which forced me to rely on filtering, loaded language, and curated comparisons to build a convincing narrative. What surprised me most was how easy it was to make the second chart look just as credible as the first, even though it's built on much shakier ground.

I believe that ethical analysis and visualization means letting the data lead the narrative rather than forcing a narrative onto the data. If I had to draw a line between acceptable persuasion and misleading choices, it would be difficult. A visualization element is misleading if it purposefully pushes a narrative that is not necessarily correct. It's more so based on intentions of harm. Therefore, it can be very difficult to detect at times. Providing evidence for a claim is something that is necessary but there are important considerations that an author needs to consider in order to prevent misinformation; however, if an author's intention is to do harm, it is not immediately clear. This is why it is very crucial for everyone to have some level of investigation skills and the ability to question their understanding.
